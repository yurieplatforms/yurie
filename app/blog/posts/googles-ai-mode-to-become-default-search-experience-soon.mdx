Google's AI Mode is poised to become the default search experience "soon," according to Logan Kilpatrick, a lead product manager at Google DeepMind, following the company's announcement of a new shortcut URL that signals the search giant's push toward artificial intelligence-powered results.

Kilpatrick confirmed the timeline on September 6, 2025, responding to user questions on social media with "soon : )" when asked about making AI Mode the standard search interface. The announcement came shortly after Google launched [google.com/ai](https://google.com/ai) as a direct shortcut to its AI Mode experience, replacing the previous google.com/aimode URL.

## Global Expansion Accelerates AI Adoption

The development follows Google's rapid expansion of AI Mode to over 180 countries and territories throughout 2025, building on earlier rollouts in the United States, United Kingdom, and India. Unlike traditional search results that display lists of website links, AI Mode provides conversational responses powered by Google's Gemini 2.5 model using a "query fan-out" technique that simultaneously processes multiple related searches to generate comprehensive answers.

Recent enhancements to AI Mode include new agent features that allow users to book restaurant reservations, schedule local service appointments, and purchase event tickets directly through the chat interface. These capabilities represent Google's shift from serving as a gateway to the web toward becoming a comprehensive platform where users can complete tasks without leaving the search experience.  

## Industry Impact and Publisher Concerns

The transition to AI-powered search as the default experience carries significant implications for website publishers and content creators. Research indicates that AI search visitors demonstrate conversion rates 4.4 times higher than traditional organic search visitors, though overall traffic volumes to external websites may decline substantially. Publishers are experiencing what industry analysts term "The Great Decoupling"—receiving more search impressions while seeing fewer actual clicks to their websites.

Google executives have acknowledged these concerns while defending the company's AI search strategy. In ongoing antitrust litigation, Google's legal team argues that "the open web is already in rapid decline," using this assertion to oppose efforts to break up the company's advertising business. Critics note the apparent contradiction between Google's claims to defend web publishers while simultaneously rolling out AI Mode that channels traffic to its own platform rather than external websites.

## Competitive Landscape

The timing of Kilpatrick's announcement coincides with increasing competitive pressure from AI-powered search alternatives including ChatGPT and Perplexity, which have gained significant user adoption with their conversational search interfaces. Google's response appears designed to maintain its search dominance by integrating similar capabilities directly into its core platform before users migrate to competing services.
