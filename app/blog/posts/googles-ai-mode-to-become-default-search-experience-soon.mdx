Google's AI Mode is poised to become the default search experience "soon," according to Logan Kilpatrick, a lead product manager at Google DeepMind. The confirmation came September 6, 2025, shortly after Google launched [google.com/ai](https://google.com/ai), a new shortcut URL that signals its push toward artificial intelligence–powered results. The update replaces the previous [google.com/aimode](https://google.com/aimode) URL.

## Global expansion accelerates adoption

Throughout 2025, Google expanded AI Mode to over 180 countries and territories, building on earlier rollouts in the U.S., U.K., and India. Unlike traditional search results that display lists of links, AI Mode provides conversational answers powered by the Gemini 2.5 model. Its "query fan-out" technique processes multiple related searches simultaneously to generate more comprehensive responses.

Recent enhancements also added **agent features**, letting users book restaurant reservations, schedule appointments, and buy event tickets directly inside the search interface. These updates shift Google from being a gateway to the web toward a platform where tasks can be completed without leaving search.

## Industry impact and publisher concerns

The move to make AI search the default has major implications for publishers and content creators. Research suggests AI search visitors convert at rates **4.4x higher** than traditional organic search traffic—but overall outbound traffic to external sites is declining. Analysts call this "The Great Decoupling": more impressions, fewer clicks.

Publishers fear being cut off from audiences as Google consolidates activity within its own ecosystem. Meanwhile, in ongoing antitrust litigation, Google's lawyers argue that "the open web is already in rapid decline," framing AI Mode as inevitable. Critics see tension between Google's claims to support publishers and its rollout of features that reduce external referrals.

## Competitive pressure drives timing

Kilpatrick’s announcement comes as conversational search challengers like **ChatGPT** and **Perplexity** gain adoption. Google’s move to integrate similar capabilities into core search appears designed to defend its dominance before users migrate to competitors.
